# Results {#sec-results}

## Cross-prompt agreement is stable across sample sizes {#sec-results-stability}

Before reporting the cross-prompt findings, the sample-size stability of the agreement metrics was verified. The filtered-median cross-prompt kappa $\bar{\kappa}^{\mathrm{TriState}}$ on the small model is {{refinement_150_kappa:.2f}} on the {{refinement_150_n}}-note refinement set, {{holdout_150_kappa:.2f}} on the {{holdout_150_n}}-note holdout set, {{methodology_1k_kappa:.2f}} on the {{methodology_1000_n}}-note validation set, {{methodology_5k_audit_kappa:.2f}} on the {{methodology_5k_audit_500_n}}-note audit subset, and {{extended_5k_kappa:.2f}} on the {{extended_5000_n}}-note extended validation set (Figure~\ref{fig:sample-size-stability}). All five point estimates sit within a narrow band. The refinement-to-holdout difference of {{refinement_holdout_delta_pp:.2f}} percentage points (Figure~\ref{fig:refinement-holdout}) is small relative to that band, consistent with the optimization loop (Section~\ref{sec-optimization-loop}) not producing a refinement-to-holdout gap. The findings reported in the subsequent sections are therefore not artifacts of any single sample size.

## Model size reshapes cross-prompt agreement rather than raising it {#sec-results-model-size}

The cross-prompt agreement at the two model sizes was evaluated on the {{methodology_1500_n}}-note paired sample, where the small model and the full model both produced extractions for the same notes under all three prompt variants (Figure~\ref{fig:cross-prompt-model-size}).

On the TriState clinical documentation flags, the larger model does not raise pooled cross-prompt agreement. The pooled-median cross-prompt kappa is {{paired_kappa_small_tristate:.2f}} at the small model (`gpt-5.4-nano`) and {{paired_kappa_full_tristate:.2f}} at the full model (`gpt-5.4`); the two levels are effectively the same. The between-model comparison is made on the paired per-field statistic rather than on the difference of these two pooled medians (Section~\ref{sec-cross-prompt-metrics}): the median over TriState fields of the per-field model-size difference $\overline{\Delta\kappa}^{\,\mathrm{per\text{-}field}}$ is {{model_size_perfield_delta_tristate:.1f}} percentage points (95% CI {{model_size_perfield_delta_tristate_ci_low_pp:.1f}} to {{model_size_perfield_delta_tristate_ci_high_pp:.1f}}). The point estimate is slightly negative and its confidence interval spans zero: under the TriState schema, the typical field is no more reproducible at the larger model, and if anything is marginally less so. Under the joint base-rate rule (at least 10 positive votes at both model sizes), `social_support_absent` is excluded from these filtered-median summaries.

The aggregate stability is not the absence of an effect. The per-field model-size difference is large and mixed in sign: the full model raises cross-prompt agreement substantially on some TriState fields and lowers it substantially on others, and these field-level changes approximately cancel in the median. {{n_fields_negative_delta_full}} of the 17 TriState fields have a negative per-field difference, meaning the small-model variants agree with each other more closely than the full-model variants do on those fields. The slightly negative median is therefore the signature of a redistribution, not a uniform decline: model size moves where in the schema the variants agree, without moving the aggregate level. The per-field distribution is the informative description of the model-size effect, and Section~\ref{sec-results-per-field} presents it in full.

The behavior under binary collapse is different in kind. When the TriState values are collapsed to a binary `yes`-versus-`not_yes` schema, the model-size effect stops cancelling and becomes consistently positive: the per-field median $\overline{\Delta\kappa}^{\,\mathrm{per\text{-}field}}$ is +{{model_size_perfield_delta_collapsed:.1f}} percentage points (95% CI {{model_size_perfield_delta_collapsed_ci_low_pp:.1f}} to {{model_size_perfield_delta_collapsed_ci_high_pp:.1f}}), and the pooled-median collapsed kappa is {{paired_kappa_small_collapsed:.2f}} at the small model and {{paired_kappa_full_collapsed:.2f}} at the full model. The contrast between the two schemas is the central observation: under the TriState schema the model-size effect cancels across fields, whereas under collapse it is positive across fields. Because the comparison uses the same notes at both model sizes, the difference reflects the change in model size and not a difference in sample composition. The per-field structure behind this contrast is developed in Section~\ref{sec-results-per-field} and its schema interpretation in Section~\ref{sec-results-collapse-structure}.

A complementary view holds each prompt variant fixed and measures how far the small-model and full-model extractions agree with each other on the same notes. This same-prompt cross-model agreement is itself low under the TriState schema: the median over TriState fields of the small-versus-full kappa is {{aa_model_size_kappa_tristate:.2f}} for variant A, {{bb_model_size_kappa_tristate:.2f}} for variant B, and {{cc_model_size_kappa_tristate:.2f}} for variant C (Figure~\ref{fig:per-variant-cross-model}). These values are well below the cross-prompt agreement at a fixed model size ({{paired_kappa_small_tristate:.2f}}--{{paired_kappa_full_tristate:.2f}}): holding the prompt fixed and changing the model size moves the TriState output more than holding the model fixed and changing the prompt does. Under binary collapse the same same-prompt cross-model agreement rises to {{aa_model_size_kappa_collapsed:.2f}} (A), {{bb_model_size_kappa_collapsed:.2f}} (B), and {{cc_model_size_kappa_collapsed:.2f}} (C), confirming that much of the small-versus-full disagreement lies on the `no`-versus-`not_documented` axis that collapse removes.

The model-size effect on the multi-class admission categorizations, shown by the two right-hand bars of Figure~\ref{fig:cross-prompt-model-size}, is of a different character and is treated separately: the larger model brings the variants markedly closer together on admission tags, and the comparison of model size against prompt phrasing on these fields is developed in Section~\ref{sec-results-tags-prevalence} and Section~\ref{sec-results-dominant-confusion}. The TriState reshaping reported here and the multi-class effect reported there are two effects of the same configuration choice on two different field types, and neither is subordinate to the other.

## The per-field model-size effect is heterogeneous in sign {#sec-results-per-field}

The per-field model-size difference distribution is the primary description of the model-size effect on TriState fields. Figure~\ref{fig:per-field-deltas} shows $\Delta\kappa_f$ for each TriState field under both schemas; the orange bars report the TriState schema and the green bars the binary-collapse re-analysis. Under the TriState schema, the per-field difference is distributed widely, from {{cardiac_rehab_delta_full:.1f}} percentage points for `cardiac_rehab_referred` to +{{cognitive_impairment_delta_full:.1f}} percentage points for `cognitive_impairment`, with the filtered-median $\overline{\Delta\kappa}^{\,\mathrm{per\text{-}field}}$ of {{model_size_perfield_delta_tristate:.1f}} percentage points sitting just left of zero. The wide, sign-mixed spread, not a uniform shift, is what the near-zero median reduces to a single number.

The sign of the median is itself informative and is easy to overlook. The typical TriState field is slightly less cross-prompt consistent at the full model than at the small model, and {{n_fields_negative_delta_full}} of the 17 fields move in that direction. Fields with strongly negative TriState $\Delta\kappa_f$ include `aki_present` ({{aki_present_delta_full:.1f}} pp), `palliative_care_consult` ({{palliative_care_delta_full:.1f}} pp), `cardiac_rehab_referred` ({{cardiac_rehab_delta_full:.1f}} pp), `shock_present` ({{shock_present_delta_full:.1f}} pp), and `home_health_ordered` ({{home_health_delta_full:.1f}} pp); on each, the small-model variants agree with each other more closely than the full-model variants do. Fields with strongly positive TriState $\Delta\kappa_f$ include `cognitive_impairment` (+{{cognitive_impairment_delta_full:.1f}} pp), `financial_hardship` (+{{financial_hardship_delta_full:.1f}} pp), and `goals_of_care_flag` (+{{goals_of_care_delta_full:.1f}} pp), where the full model brings the variants closer together.

Read together with the collapse result from Section~\ref{sec-results-model-size}, these two facts describe a single mechanism. The per-field median is slightly negative under the TriState schema ({{model_size_perfield_delta_tristate:.1f}} pp) and clearly positive under collapse (+{{model_size_perfield_delta_collapsed:.1f}} pp). Model size therefore does not raise cross-prompt agreement uniformly; it redistributes it. The larger model improves agreement on the presence-versus-absence distinction, which is the part that survives collapse, while on the same fields it slightly worsens agreement on the `no`-versus-`not_documented` distinction, which is the part collapse removes. The aggregate level holds roughly constant because these two movements offset.

## Binary collapse isolates a schema-imposed component of disagreement {#sec-results-collapse-structure}

The binary-collapse re-analysis varies one configuration choice, schema granularity, while holding the prompts, the model, and the extracted values fixed. Comparing the TriState and collapsed agreement on the same extractions isolates the part of cross-prompt disagreement that is attributable to the three-way schema from the part that is not. The per-field median $\Delta\kappa_f$ moves from {{model_size_perfield_delta_tristate:.1f}} percentage points under the TriState schema to +{{model_size_perfield_delta_collapsed:.1f}} percentage points under collapse (Figure~\ref{fig:per-field-deltas}). Under the TriState schema the model-size effect is heterogeneous in sign and cancels in aggregate; under collapse it is consistently positive across fields.

The pattern of per-field swings is the informative part. Fields with strongly negative TriState $\Delta\kappa_f$ all flip to positive under collapse: `cardiac_rehab_referred` from {{cardiac_rehab_delta_full:.1f}} to +{{cardiac_rehab_delta_collapsed:.1f}} pp, `aki_present` from {{aki_present_delta_full:.1f}} to +{{aki_present_delta_collapsed:.1f}} pp, and `palliative_care_consult` from {{palliative_care_delta_full:.1f}} to +{{palliative_care_delta_collapsed:.1f}} pp. On these fields the small-model variants disagreed primarily on the `no`-versus-`not_documented` axis. Collapse merges those two values into one class, that axis of disagreement disappears, and the small-model kappa rises sharply; the full-model kappa, already high under TriState because the full model rarely confuses `no` and `not_documented`, rises only modestly. The per-field difference therefore flips from negative to positive. Fields with positive TriState $\Delta\kappa_f$ keep their direction under collapse but shrink in magnitude (`cognitive_impairment` from +{{cognitive_impairment_delta_full:.1f}} to +{{cognitive_impairment_delta_collapsed:.1f}} pp, `goals_of_care_flag` from +{{goals_of_care_delta_full:.1f}} to +{{goals_of_care_delta_collapsed:.1f}} pp), because on these fields the disagreement was already on the surviving presence-versus-absence axis.

The per-pair view in Figure~\ref{fig:per-pair-kappa} confirms the pattern at finer resolution. Its third panel, the per-cell difference $\kappa_{p,f}^{\mathrm{collapsed}} - \kappa_{p,f}^{\mathrm{TriState}}$, shows where collapse changes agreement most. The largest positive differences appear on `cognitive_impairment`, `shock_present`, and `cardiac_rehab_referred`, where collapse raises every variant pair's kappa substantially. The smallest appear on `substance_use_active`, `fall_risk_documented`, and `home_health_ordered`, where collapse changes kappa by only a few points for all pairs. A small number of fields show negative differences (`discharge_delayed_reason`, `social_support_absent`, `dnr_dni_documented`), where collapse removes signal from variant pairs that were using the three-way structure successfully. This per-field asymmetry, rather than the aggregate dissolution alone, is what distinguishes a schema-imposed component from a generic coarsening effect, and it is the empirical basis for the interpretation in Section~\ref{sec-discussion-two-sources}.

## Most TriState disagreement is on the soft axis {#sec-results-soft-disagreement}

Decomposing cross-variant TriState disagreement by type shows where the disagreement sits (Figure~\ref{fig:disagreement-decomposition}). Almost all of it is soft, meaning at least one variant assigned `not_documented` while another assigned a definite value: soft disagreements are {{disagreement_soft_pct:.1f}}% of all cross-variant TriState disagreements, and hard `yes`-versus-`no` flips, where the variants assert opposite definite values, are the remaining {{disagreement_hard_pct:.1f}}%. The soft total splits into two sub-axes: {{disagreement_soft_no_vs_not_documented_pct:.1f}}% of all disagreements are `no` versus `not_documented`, and {{disagreement_soft_yes_vs_not_documented_pct:.1f}}% are `yes` versus `not_documented`. The single largest component is therefore the `no`-versus-`not_documented` axis, the distinction between an explicit negation and silence.

Binary collapse acts precisely on that largest component. Aggregated across all TriState fields on the {{cross_variant_pooled_n}}-note cross-variant pool, the total disagreement count is {{disagreement_count_full_tristate}} under the TriState schema and {{disagreement_count_collapsed}} under collapse, so {{disagreement_dissolved_pct:.1f}}% of the disagreement dissolves when `no` and `not_documented` are merged. This dissolved fraction is the same quantity as the `no`-versus-`not_documented` share above, since those are exactly the disagreements collapse removes; it is one number seen two ways rather than two findings. The residual {{disagreement_residual_pct:.1f}}% is the disagreement that survives collapse, on the `yes`-versus-not-yes axis. Most of the apparent cross-variant disagreement on TriState fields is thus located on the schema-imposed soft axis, and only a minority reflects disagreement about whether the feature is present at all.

## Prompt phrasing shifts multi-class admission categorization {#sec-results-tags-prevalence}

The admission-tag prevalence by variant (Figure~\ref{fig:tag-prevalence}, panel A) shows that the three variants categorize admission reasons at systematically different rates. Variant C assigns `symptom_workup_other` on {{c_symptom_workup_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% of notes, against {{a_symptom_workup_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% for variant A and {{b_symptom_workup_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% for variant B. Variant A assigns `infection_other` on {{a_infection_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% of notes, against {{b_infection_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% (B) and {{c_infection_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% (C). The clearest contrast is on the generic `other` tag: variant C reaches for it on {{c_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% of notes, well below variant A ({{a_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}%) and variant B ({{b_other_prevalence_pct:.1f}}%). Variant C is the most specific of the three, resorting to the undifferentiated catch-all least often.

Because the variants emit different numbers of tags per note (means {{mean_tags_per_note_a:.2f}} for A, {{mean_tags_per_note_b:.2f}} for B, {{mean_tags_per_note_c:.2f}} for C), panel B normalizes each tag's count by the variant's total tag firings. The differences persist after normalization: variant C's share of `symptom_workup_other` firings is {{c_symptom_workup_other_normalized_share_pct:.1f}}% against {{a_symptom_workup_other_normalized_share_pct:.1f}}% (A) and {{b_symptom_workup_other_normalized_share_pct:.1f}}% (B), so the categorization differences are not an artifact of total tag count. The differences are consistent in direction across the validation samples; each variant makes internally consistent but phrasing-dependent categorization choices, and different phrasings push the model toward different categorical readings of the same clinical content.

The same specificity gradient appears as a model-size effect, and it sharpens under the larger model. On the {{methodology_1500_n}}-note paired sample, with variant outputs averaged, the share of tag mass assigned to residual and catch-all categories (the `other` tag together with the `*_other` family) falls from {{residual_mass_small_pct:.1f}}% at the small model to {{residual_mass_full_pct:.1f}}% at the full model, driven mostly by the `other` tag itself dropping from {{other_tag_mass_small_pct:.1f}}% to {{other_tag_mass_full_pct:.1f}}%. The mass moves into more specific categories, for example `elective_procedure_non_oncology` ({{elective_procedure_non_oncology_mass_small_pct:.1f}}% to {{elective_procedure_non_oncology_mass_full_pct:.1f}}%), `heme_onc_complication` ({{heme_onc_complication_mass_small_pct:.1f}}% to {{heme_onc_complication_mass_full_pct:.1f}}%), and `trauma_fracture` ({{trauma_fracture_mass_small_pct:.1f}}% to {{trauma_fracture_mass_full_pct:.1f}}%). Per-assignment confusion on residual categories also falls at the full model for most of these tags, for example `neuro_other` ({{neuro_other_confusion_rate_small_pct:.0f}}% to {{neuro_other_confusion_rate_full_pct:.0f}}%) and `gi_other` ({{gi_other_confusion_rate_small_pct:.0f}}% to {{gi_other_confusion_rate_full_pct:.0f}}%), though not uniformly: `cardiac_other` and `trauma_other` are flat or slightly higher. The full model thus categorizes admission reasons more specifically on two counts, placing less mass on catch-all categories and confusing the catch-all categories it does use somewhat less often.

## Model size moves the dominant admission reason more than phrasing does {#sec-results-dominant-confusion}

The single-choice primary admission reason (Figure~\ref{fig:admission-confusion}) is the field on which the model-size and phrasing effects can be compared most directly, because both reduce to one quantity: the fraction of notes on which the dominant tag changes. Holding the model fixed and changing only the prompt, the cross-variant dominant-tag agreement at the full model is high, {{ab_full_dominant_diagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (A-B), {{ac_full_dominant_diagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (A-C), and {{bc_full_dominant_diagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (B-C), so prompt phrasing changes the dominant tag on roughly one note in eight. Holding the prompt fixed and changing only the model size moves it far more often: the same-prompt cross-model dominant-tag confusion has off-diagonal mass of {{aa_model_size_offdiagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (variant A), {{bb_model_size_offdiagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (variant B), and {{cc_model_size_offdiagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (variant C) on the {{methodology_1500_n}}-note paired sample, meaning the small and full models select a different dominant admission tag on close to half of all notes even when the prompt is identical (the full $47\times47$ same-prompt cross-model confusion matrices are given in supplementary Figures~\ref{fig:cross-model-a}, \ref{fig:cross-model-b}, and \ref{fig:cross-model-c}). For these three variants and two model operating points, changing the model size is several times more consequential for the dominant admission reason than changing the prompt phrasing. This is the multi-class counterpart of the TriState reshaping in Section~\ref{sec-results-model-size}: model size is the dominant configuration choice on admission-reason categorization, just as it redistributes agreement on the TriState fields.

At the small model, cross-variant agreement on the dominant tag is {{ab_dominant_diagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (A-B), {{ac_dominant_diagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (A-C), and {{bc_dominant_diagonal_mass_pct:.1f}}% (B-C); variants A and B are the most consistent pair and A and C the least. The off-diagonal mass concentrates in interpretable clusters (Figure~\ref{fig:admission-confusion}): `obstetric` versus `other`, where variants disagree about whether to tag narrowly or under the catch-all; `cardiac_other` versus `cardiac_valve_disease`, where the two cardiac categories are not cleanly separated; `symptom_workup_other` versus `infection_other`, where the same content is routed to a workup category by one variant and an infection category by another. The variants are not merely labeling at different rates but routing the same admissions to different categorical destinations.

This disagreement is not spread evenly across the vocabulary. On the pooled {{cross_variant_pooled_n}}-note small-model sample, the per-tag confusion mass scales strongly with per-tag prevalence: Spearman's correlation between the two, averaged across the three variant pairs, is $\rho = {{tag_confusion_prevalence_spearman_rho:.2f}}$ over the 47 tags. The most-confused tags are the common ones, dominated by residual or catch-all categories, and the least-confused are rare conditions with sharp diagnostic anchors. Beyond this prevalence scaling, the per-assignment confusion rate, confusion mass divided by prevalence, is substantially higher for residual categories such as `other` and the `*_other` family than for sharply defined categories such as diabetic ketoacidosis, COPD or asthma exacerbation, and pulmonary embolism or deep vein thrombosis. Cross-variant disagreement on the dominant admission reason therefore concentrates on the categories without clear diagnostic anchors, where the prompt's phrasing decides which of several plausible labels is applied.

## Enum field agreement is high with category-specific residual confusion {#sec-results-enum}

The enum fields with closed value sets show high cross-variant agreement overall, with structured residual confusion that varies by field. Figure~\ref{fig:enum-mental-status}, Figure~\ref{fig:enum-functional-status}, and Figure~\ref{fig:enum-discharge-condition} report the variant-pair confusion structure for mental status, functional status at discharge, and discharge condition category respectively, on the pooled {{cross_variant_pooled_n}}-note cross-variant sample.

Functional status at discharge is the most consistent enum field, with diagonal mass {{enum_functional_status_ab_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (A-B), {{enum_functional_status_ac_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (A-C), and {{enum_functional_status_bc_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (B-C). The four-class scheme of `independent`, `assisted`, `dependent`, and `not_documented` is reproduced consistently across variants, and the only off-diagonal cluster at appreciable scale is in the `dependent` row, where a minority of `dependent` notes by one variant are read as `assisted` by another.

Mental status shows a similar overall level, with diagonal mass {{enum_mental_status_ab_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (A-B), {{enum_mental_status_ac_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (A-C), and {{enum_mental_status_bc_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (B-C). Off-diagonal mass concentrates between `mild_impairment` and `confused_delirious`, and between `not_documented` and `intact`. The `not_documented` row is variant-pair-specific: the A-B pair keeps most `not_documented`-by-A notes as `not_documented`-by-B, whereas a smaller fraction of `not_documented`-by-B notes remain `not_documented`-by-C, with a sizeable share read as `intact` instead. Variant C appears to treat the absence of an explicit cognitive assessment as evidence of an intact baseline more often than variant B does.

Discharge condition category shows the lowest agreement of the three, with diagonal mass {{enum_discharge_condition_ab_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (A-B), {{enum_discharge_condition_ac_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (A-C), and {{enum_discharge_condition_bc_diagonal_pct:.1f}}% (B-C). Off-diagonal mass concentrates between `unchanged` and the more clinically specific categories `stable`, `improved`, and `deteriorated`, suggesting that `unchanged` functions as a default that the variants invoke at different rates. The `deteriorated` row shows substantial mass into `improved` and `not_documented`, indicating that the categorization of clinical decline is variant-dependent, while `expired` is essentially identical across variants because the underlying fact is unambiguous in the source. The pattern across the three fields is consistent with the broader findings: agreement is high where the categories are concrete and observable, and the residual disagreement concentrates on interpretive category boundaries.

## Labeling-function and LLM signals are complementary {#sec-results-lf-llm}

The labeling-function ensemble provides an independent reference signal against which the LLM extraction can be checked target by target, and the relationship between the two is not uniform across targets (Figures~\ref{fig:lf-icd-concordance}, \ref{fig:lf-regex-concordance}, and \ref{fig:aki-five-signal}).

For some targets, ICD codes capture broadly what the LLM extracts. On acute kidney injury, the LLM-positive prevalence across variants ranges from {{aki_llm_c_prevalence_pct:.1f}}% to {{aki_llm_a_prevalence_pct:.1f}}%, against an ICD-anchor prevalence of {{aki_icd_lf_prevalence_pct:.1f}}%, with variant-versus-ICD kappa in the {{aki_llm_vs_icd_kappa_a:.2f}} to {{aki_llm_vs_icd_kappa_b:.2f}} range. The five-signal pool of three LLM variants, the ICD anchor, and the regex anchor on $n = {{cross_variant_pooled_n}}$ notes contains {{aki_all_signals_negative_count}} notes where no signal fires, {{aki_icd_only_no_llm_count}} where ICD fires but no LLM variant does, and {{aki_all_llm_positive_no_icd_count}} where all three LLM variants fire but ICD does not. That ICD-only-no-LLM cases outnumber all-LLM-no-ICD cases is informative about each signal's coverage.

For other targets the asymmetry runs the other way. On cardiac heart failure, the probability that the LLM fires given that ICD fires is high ($P(\mathrm{LLM} \mid \mathrm{ICD}) = {{cardiac_hf_p_llm_yes_given_icd:.2f}}$), while the reverse is lower ($P(\mathrm{ICD} \mid \mathrm{LLM}) = {{cardiac_hf_p_icd_given_llm_yes:.2f}}$). On trauma fracture the probabilities flip, with ICD capturing fractures broadly and the LLM flagging them narrowly ($P(\mathrm{LLM} \mid \mathrm{ICD}) = {{trauma_fracture_p_llm_yes_given_icd:.2f}}$, $P(\mathrm{ICD} \mid \mathrm{LLM}) = {{trauma_fracture_p_icd_given_llm_yes:.2f}}$). These asymmetries are interpretable in clinical-practice terms (Section~\ref{sec-discussion-implications}): ICD captures billing-relevant codes that need not be the dominant admission reason, while the LLM captures content prominent in the discharge narrative, so disagreement between the signals is informative rather than purely noisy.

Regex labeling functions have high specificity but limited coverage. Regex prevalence on `aki_present` is {{aki_regex_lf_prevalence_pct:.1f}}%, well below the LLM and ICD prevalence on the same target, so regex labels serve as a high-precision anchor rather than a primary reference signal.
